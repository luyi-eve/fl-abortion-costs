How Much It Takes To Get An Abortion In The 'PURPLE' State Of Florida?

A deep analysis of nonmedical costs on transportation and lodging expenses for an abortion round trip in Florida.

By Yi (Eve) Lu

October 2022

After the U.S. Supreme Court's decision to overturn Roe v. Wade, most states in the Deep South have been criminalizing abortion or have enacted other gestational limits on the procedure. A new Florida law of a 15-week ban with limited exceptions passed this July now goes into effect. That said, Florida still comes into view as a haven for abortion access for people in the Southeast as its neighboring state Georgia now makes abortion illegal as early as six weeks of gestational age while Alabama turns abortion into a completely unlawful performance.





15-week Ban Now Becomes A Haven

In 2021, there were 7,9817 abortions performed in Florida according to AHCA, and 4,873 of the total cases were reported by residents out of state. The number has been climbing to its climax in the recent three years.

Currently, there are 54 abortion providers in Florida which turn out to be a sharp number compared to Alabama and Georgia as many facilities in neighboring states close or stop providing abortion services due to statewide law enforcement.

However, are those facilities able to meet the demand of Florida's nearly 3.9 million women of childbearing age [1]?

Of all 67 counties in Florida, only 15 of them have accessibility to abortion services, which has left most northern and central parts of the state as the wasteland of women's reproductive rights.

In North Florida, Duval has four abortion facilities in total located in the Northeastern corner of Jacksonville, a city that is considered to be the most populous area on the Atlantic coast of Florida, while only two are detected in the county of Alachua.

However, abortion becomes harder to obtain when it goes deeper into the Northwestern part of the state. Only two facilities are available in Leon, a county that is geographically closest to the tripoint of the three states. The local abortion business may become overwhelmed not only with patients from its nearby counties but from Alabama and Georgia.

In Central Florida, patients living in counties such as Pinellas or Hillsborough are much luckier because they have more choices of different abortion providers, up to 11 in total than those living in Orange, Seminole, Osceola, or Polk, with only one facility being detected per county.

In South Florida, Miami-Dade County with the highest number of 16 facilities can be viewed as the most abortion-friendly region across the state, followed by seven in Broward and three in Palm Beach.

The situation seems to be acceptable and less severe than in other states when people simply look at the number of facilities in certain counties in Florida. However, geographic accessibility doesn't bring along a panacea for all concerns about abortion. The confrontation with the anxiety at the overall less secure abortion protection, along with the following financial struggles by patients traveling farther to seek abortion care, are awaiting all women.

In Florida, in particular, if a woman aims for an abortion, she must receive an in-person state-directed counseling that includes information designed to discourage herself from the procedure and then undergoes a 24-hour waiting period before having an abortion. Thereby, at least two trips to the facility are required by law, which might enforce extra expenses for long-distance travel on patients regarding to transportation and lodging choices.

Northern Floridians Are Traveling Farther

To quantify access to the facility by measuring what people really care about: how far it takes to drive there and how much should be paid for gas. Let's start by looking at the distance to the nearest clinic for different counties in Florida.

Escambia is on the westernmost corner of Florida. In May 2021, the only operating abortion clinic in this county was ordered to close after three patients were hospitalized, which has made Escambia the farthest county to get abortion cares in the state.

To get to its nearest facility in Tallahassee, patients in Escambia need to travel about 362 miles for a round trip across 13 counties all the way down to Leon where only two facilities are detected. Possible gas fees and extra lodging costs shall be taken into account if patients do not wish to return and make the same 4-hour trip again as 24 hours between the first counseling and the actual procedure are mandated by Florida's law.

This long-distance travel from Escambia to Tallahassee, takes an estimated six to eight hours' drive for a round, which roughly costs about $50 of gas price to run the entire trip.

Patients traveling from Northern counties such as Santa Rosa, Okaloosa and Walton have to pay more for gasoline supply than South because of the longer distances. In Central Florida, accessible facilities are also limited since patients over there heavily rely on the abortion business at Pinellas and Hillsborough where there are more choices of facilities but they may not always be the nearest ones to the county they live in.

In addition, to plan the logistics of such a trip, occasionally, patients will require a hotel nearby the facility. For instance, if a woman travels from Escambia to its nearest facility in Leon, the median cost she has to pay for a hotel is $99 per night. With that being said, the cost varies by multiple factors including the county's population, whether the facilities are located in the downtown areas, and during which seasons to go, peak or low, will also contribute to a wide range of costs on lodging.

Staying Longer Requires More Money

Filter by the median lodging cost to see traveling to which facilities makes you pay for the hotels at a different price. Hover over a dot for more details.

Osceola, where the median cost for a hotel per night is $65, has the most affordable hotels around the only facility in its place across the state, while hotels in Sarasota around the services are more expensive at which the median hotel prices can reach up to $223 for a one-night stay.

In short, it is always your ZIP Code that decides how far you drive and how much you pay. If patients wish to have more choices when selecting a safe abortion provider with a cozy and pleasant hotel to stay in afterwards, they will have to throw more dollars for exploration on the road than others. For instance, Miami-Dade County has the most well-developed abortion business where 16 facilities are detected, but it is also a place where the luxury hotel industry remains remarkably popular and commonly distributed throughout the region. There is even no such price cap for how much a hotel can cost up to in Miami.

The overall median lodging price for abortion in Florida is $118 per night. However, after the Court's decision, patients will only travel farther and stay longer to seek accessible and secure abortion care. Money spent on lodging is only a small perceptible part of a much larger additional nonmedical expenditure on the entire process including childcare, meal expenses and even flights. Thousands of dollars can also be spent on the procedure itself as a result of abortion care often being excluded from major health insurance plans.

The cost of an abortion can vary depending on the state, the facility, the patient's health insurance, and mainly on the type of procedure that the patient chooses. If without any insurance coverage, the average cost of either surgical or medical for first-trimester abortions at 10 weeks of pregnancy can cost more than $500[2], while later terms even reach up to $2,000 or higher.

However, not every woman is privileged to afford a round trip to get an abortion. There are over 866,000 [3] nonelderly women living below the poverty line in Florida, and these major costs can bring heavy financial distress for low-income women without sufficient funding support.

To receive health coverage through Florida Medicaid[4], a family of three's monthly income ceiling is $2,552.5 [5], but adhering to the Hyde Amendment requirements[6], publicly funded insurance plans like FL Medicaid are restricted from covering abortion services, only except in the cases of life endangerment, rape, or incest, which indicates that if a woman is at the higher end of FL Medicaid eligibility, what her family has to pay for the termination of a later term in pregnancy would take nearly half of their monthly income. Therefore, in the wild world, it is very possible for a woman who is forced to delay the procedure for not having raised enough money, which might also bring about a higher risk of other gynecological problems.

In Justice Alito's words, the decision returns the authority to regulate abortion to the people and their elected representatives. However, ending Roe is not just a matter of eliminating the constitutional right and giving more freedom back to the states to let them decide what they want to do in terms of abortion. It is a matter of taking away women's right to receive proper health care and handing it in to those who have the power to make decisions for their own sake. It is a matter of disregarding the structural disparities of gender, race and social class and reinforcing a pseudo-democracy society where no real choices are directed to its citizens.

Ending Roe will never succeed in ending abortion, it will only succeed in ending safe and affordable abortion.

Methodology

54 abortion providers Data for this section are collected and documented by me. I created a statewide dataset to track open and accessible abortion providers in Florida in 2022. This dataset identifies the names, addresses, coordinates, and counties of all facilities that publicly advertised their provision of abortion services and care. This dataset is not intended to identify whether the facility is a clinic, hospital, or physician's office and does not capture the facilities that only provide a small number of abortions every year.

I detect the facilities by using a variety of sources for cross-checking purposes. These include state archive records and Facility Locator Service supported by the Florida Agency for Health Care Administration (AHCA); current directories of National Abortion Federal (NAF) members; current directories of Planned Parenthood health centers; several non-profit based online abortion provider search tools such as Abortion Finder, Ineedana, etc.; and I also use Google Map to double-check whether the facility is closed or still in service. If it mentions 'permanently close' on its profile, I call the facility to ensure their availability. By using this dataset my goals are:

1) To recognize the distance between each county's center and its nearest facility;
2) To calculate the gas price for a round trip based on the distance to each county's nearest facility;
3) To explore how many hotels are around each facility and the potential lodging costs.

Distances to the nearest facility To acquire the data in terms of distance, I followed the rule that was used by NYT in its abortion coverage â€“ distance is calculated from the center of the county to its nearest facility. Since I already have the data of the facilities' coordinates, for this section, I locate the centroid of each county by using Geometry Tools in QGIS to generate the coordinates of the county's center and then calculate the geodesic distance by introducing the Haversine Distance formula with Python. Finally, by comparing the distance between the county's center and each facility, I am able to identify the nearest one and the distance between two geo-locations.

Gas prices to the nearest facility Data in this section are calculated based on the Florida average regular gas price updated and published by The AAA Gas Prices website. However, given the fact that the gas price slightly fluctuates on a daily basis, I rounded the regular gas price to $3.3 per gallon after observing the current, yesterday, weekly, monthly, and yearly average regular price whose number in tenth place stably remains at three.

Hotel cost for each facility As I mentioned in the story, the lodging cost is not a stable expenditure. It is impacted by multiple realistic factors and gets changed on a daily basis depending on the time when people order a reservation, the location of the hotel itself, and the population of the county. In this case, the data I present here has its limitation and only intend to provide a general sense to my readers of how much the hotels around the facility would cost for a one-night stay. In order to get the data of prices, I identify the hotels on Google Maps and rely on its algorithm that is set up to display the price depending on the availability of different hotels. Below are the major steps for the data collection:

1) From Google Maps, I collected over 3000 URLs of the hotels by using paid service Outscraper along with some manual work to obtain the information of available hotels around each facility.

2) Then, I analyzed and scraped these URLs in two continuous days in early October by using Selenium to further collect the prices. However, please note that the dates of the scraped prices are not the same days, and they may differ from each other due to different hotels' availability. Originally, I was planning to scrape the prices in one single day to avoid even slight fluctuation in the prices. However, due to a large amount of data, I could only manage it in two continuous days. Also, the scraped prices shouldn't be referred to imply the costs during any federal holidays or peak tourism seasons in Florida.
